An Essay on Amateur Radio & the Uncorrupted Commons

The Uncorrupted Signal

On Why Amateur Radio Endures and Why It Matters

By Malcolm Devine, KE2IBV  ·  In collaboration with Claude (Anthropic)  ·  2025

A note on authorship This essay was developed in conversation between a sixty-seven-year-old newly licensed amateur radio operator — holding a Technician ticket for approximately one month — and Claude, an AI system made by Anthropic. The lived experience, the instincts, the pushback and the editorial judgement belong to the human. The AI contributed organisational capacity and helped articulate what the human was circling but hadn't yet landed. Neither produced this alone; neither could have. That collaboration is itself part of what the essay is about, and we will return to it.

Why does amateur radio exist? Not in the technical sense — the physics are well understood, the regulatory framework is clear, the history is documented. The question is philosophical. In a world saturated with communication alternatives, where a device in every pocket connects any human to any other human at essentially zero marginal cost and zero technical effort, why does a hobby that requires a federal licence, genuine technical knowledge, and a real investment of time and money not only survive but sustain millions of practitioners worldwide — including, on Long Island alone, more than a dozen active clubs?

The easy answer is emergency preparedness. When the infrastructure fails, the argument goes, ham radio operators are there. This is true and it matters, but it is an instrumental case, not a principled one. It tells you amateur radio is useful in extremis; it does not tell you why it is worth practising on an ordinary Tuesday evening. There is a better answer, and it begins with something so fundamental that it is easy to overlook.

Amateur radio is, at its core, a person talking to another person. Not through a platform. Not via infrastructure owned and operated by a corporation whose interests are structurally misaligned with the people using it. Not as a data point in someone's advertising model or an inventory unit on someone's balance sheet. One human being, with a radio they understand and often built themselves, reaching through the electromagnetic spectrum to find another human being willing to respond. That is what it was when Marconi's contemporaries first began experimenting with wireless telegraphy in the early twentieth century. It is what it remains today. The medium has evolved considerably; the essence has not moved at all.

This sounds simple. It is not simple. In the current communication landscape, it is almost miraculous.

✦

Every major communication platform of the last thirty years has undergone what the writer Cory Doctorow has termed enshittification — a predictable process by which platforms are first good to users, then extract value from users to serve business customers, then extract from everyone to serve shareholders, degrading steadily until they are barely recognisable as the things they began as. Email became spam. Search became advertising. Social networks became surveillance engines optimised for outrage. The postal service, that earlier marvel of democratic communication, was enshittified by bulk mail long before the internet existed, its public mission gradually subordinated to the interests of its largest customers and, in time, to the institutional interests of its own workforce. The mission became secondary to the apparatus that had grown up around it.

Amateur radio cannot undergo this process. The prohibition is not cultural — not a norm that goodwill sustains and cynicism can eventually erode — but structural and legal. An amateur licence cannot be used for commercial purposes. This is not a guideline or a best practice; it is a licence condition, backed by federal communications law. The mechanism by which every other medium has been corrupted — the insertion of commercial interest between communicating humans — is structurally unavailable here. The community did not need to defend against enshittification because the architecture made enshittification impossible.

One century of continuous practice. Structurally identical in its human character to what it was at the beginning. It is genuinely difficult to name another institution of comparable age and scale of which the same can honestly be said.
✦

The economist Elinor Ostrom spent her career documenting something that conventional economics had declared impossible: communities successfully governing shared resources — fisheries, forests, irrigation systems — without either privatisation or heavy state intervention. She won the Nobel Prize for it in 2009, partly because her empirical findings directly contradicted the prevailing assumption that commons were doomed to overexploitation. The design features she identified as making commons governance durable include clear membership boundaries, community rule-making authority, self-monitoring, and graduated sanctions for rule-breakers.

Amateur radio's use of the electromagnetic spectrum is precisely the kind of commons she was describing, and it exhibits every one of those features. The licence is the membership boundary — not arbitrary but earned, through demonstrated engagement with the underlying science. The band plans that govern who transmits where and when are largely community-developed conventions rather than federal mandates. The monitoring is performed by operators themselves; the community polices its own spectrum with an effectiveness that no government agency could replicate. The FCC sets the framework and holds ultimate authority, but the examining system is run entirely by volunteer hams, and the social enforcement — the community's own pressure on bad actors — does most of the practical daily work.

This is not prisoners watched by guards. It is practitioners who understand the resource they share, have a direct interest in protecting it, and do so voluntarily. Ostrom never examined amateur radio; her empirical work was grounded in physical resource commons where you can count fish and measure water. But the framework maps onto the amateur spectrum so cleanly that it reads as a vindication of her theory in a domain she never reached. That is more useful, in an argument, than a direct citation would have been.

✦

Entry to this commons is meaningful precisely because it is earned. Every amateur operator has passed through a threshold that required genuine engagement with electromagnetic theory, regulation, and operating practice. There are no passive users, no accidental participants, no one who arrived because an algorithm served them a recommendation. Every person on the air made a deliberate choice, and the community that results is shaped profoundly by that self-selection. You are among people who are there on purpose; that changes the texture of everything.

The callsign that every licensed operator carries is a specific and unusual kind of identity. It is publicly registered, globally traceable, and permanently accountable — yet it carries no brand, no persona, no follower count. It is the inverse of social media identity in an important respect: maximum accountability, zero performance incentive. There is nothing to optimise toward except the actual thing you are doing. The ionosphere is not interested in your personal brand.

This creates something that the current communication environment has largely engineered out of existence: authenticity in the philosophically precise sense, not as an aesthetic but as a structural condition. The contact either happened or it did not. The signal reached or it did not. No amount of curation or performance changes the physics.

✦

The physics themselves are worth pausing on. Every other major communication technology has been an exercise in progressive abstraction — peeling the user further from the physical reality of the signal. You press send; something happens; someone receives it. The infrastructure, the propagation, the electromagnetic reality are all invisible by design, because visibility is friction and friction is the enemy of scale.

Amateur radio inverts this entirely. The operator is the infrastructure. Understanding the physics is not optional background knowledge; it is the practice itself. When an HF operator makes contact with a station on the other side of the world, they are acutely aware that the ionosphere may or may not cooperate, that their signal is a physical thing travelling through a physical world with its own conditions and moods. That awareness restores a weight to the act of communication that modern infrastructure has deliberately optimised away. The medium is not transparent; it is present. And that presence — that sense of genuinely reaching through something real — confers meaning on the exchange before a word is spoken.

Amateur radio is not, it should be said, hostile to newer technology. It has absorbed digital modes, satellite communication, internet linking, and software-defined radio with genuine enthusiasm. Operators who have been at it for seventy years have watched the technological world transform beyond recognition around a practice that remained philosophically constant. They adapted the tools without losing the essence. That is not stubbornness; it is a kind of wisdom about what actually matters. The digital sits on top of the physics. It does not replace the relationship with it.

There is also, in this relationship with the physical, a dimension of silence and patience that the rest of the communication landscape has declared obsolete. Ham radio still has waiting — waiting for conditions to improve, for a contact to emerge from noise, for the ionosphere to cooperate. A significant part of operating is simply listening: being present and attentive to the spectrum without transmitting. In a media environment built around the compulsion to broadcast oneself, and engineered to eliminate every moment of friction between impulse and transmission, that contemplative quality is not a deficiency that technology has not yet corrected. It is a different and valuable relationship with attention itself.

On the air, an interesting thing happens to the categories that divide people elsewhere. The practice tends to produce civility — not the forced politeness of managed interactions, and not the absence of human prejudice, which exists here as everywhere, but something older and more substantive: the behaviour appropriate to people sharing a common purpose. You identify yourself, you acknowledge the other station, you complete the exchange, you sign off. That ritual has a civilising effect that requires nothing of the operator except the desire to make the contact. This stands in direct contrast to the dominant communication platforms of our time, whose architecture actively rewards the opposite — combative, tribal behaviour, because outrage generates engagement and engagement is the commodity being extracted. Amateur radio has no such incentive and no such mechanism. In a public discourse that has largely abandoned civility as either naive or inconvenient, there is something genuinely striking about a practice that produces it structurally, without instruction or enforcement.

Honesty requires going further, though. Civility on the air does not mean the community is without its failures. There are operators who are unwelcoming, cliquish, or worse. The structure keeps a lid on the worst of it in the moments that matter most — in the exchange itself — but it does not reach into the culture around the practice, into forums, into club meetings, into the assumptions about who belongs here and who doesn't. The demographics tell part of that story plainly: the community is predominantly older and predominantly male, and a newcomer today is statistically likely to encounter exactly that.

The harder structural problem runs deeper. The callsign accountability we rightly admire — maximum transparency, no anonymity — is simultaneously a real barrier for people who have learned through experience that being publicly identifiable and locatable carries personal risk. A callsign ties to a name and an address in a publicly searchable database. For a young woman engaging with strangers across a global network, that is not an abstract concern. The feature that functions as an antidote to social media's toxic anonymity creates, for some, a legitimate reason for caution. Nothing in the architecture requires the community to look the way it does. The structural properties we have been examining are neutral with respect to age, gender and background. The gap between those principles and the present reality is not a reason to dismiss the philosophy. It is an honest challenge to live up to it.

On a good day on the HF bands, you might speak with someone in Japan, someone in Brazil, and someone in Finland — not because an algorithm decided to show you those people, but because physics and skill made the contact possible. The randomness is not a bug. It is one of the medium's quiet virtues.

The clubs that sustain amateur radio at the local level deserve a word, because they are themselves philosophically interesting. On Long Island alone there are more than a dozen, from general-purpose clubs to specialist groups dedicated to low-power operation or the revival of Morse code. Annual dues can be as low as forty dollars. Most do not require membership to attend meetings or to use repeater systems that cost real time and real money to install and maintain. Visitors are welcomed without condition. When operators travel, they can walk into a club meeting in a strange city and find immediate common ground; the licence is the passport, the callsign the introduction. It is a worldwide fellowship sustained by nothing more than shared practice and mutual goodwill, and it has functioned this way, without a head office extracting value upward, for a century.

Knowledge moves through this fellowship via what operators call the Elmer tradition — experienced practitioners mentoring newcomers without payment, credential, or institutional framework. It is knowledge transfer as pure community value, with no platform taking a percentage and no algorithm deciding who gets to learn from whom. The same tradition may also be the most powerful instrument available for closing the gap between the community's stated principles and its present demographics — not through institutional programmes, which tend to signal virtue more reliably than they create belonging, but person to person, one conversation at a time. These are questions worth asking. This essay is not the place to answer them.

A final word on how this essay came to exist, because the how is part of the argument. It was developed in conversation between a sixty-seven-year-old newly licensed amateur radio operator and Claude, an AI system made by Anthropic. The human brought a month's operating experience, a lifetime of thinking about institutions and technology, the instincts that recognised which ideas were alive, and the editorial judgement to know when something was right. The AI brought organisational capacity and the ability to articulate what the human was circling. The essay you have read is the product of genuine dialogue between them.

There is a light irony in this that we acknowledge freely. A piece partly authored by the most advanced conversational AI currently available, arguing for the irreplaceable value of direct human communication across physical distance, written by someone who has been licensed for four weeks, addressed in part to operators who have been pounding the brass for seven decades. That is not a contradiction. It is a demonstration of the argument.

Because the juxtaposition of old and new, human and machine, deep experience and fresh eyes, is not a problem to be resolved. It is what genuine exchange looks like. The medium changes. The impulse to reach across distance, to make contact with another human being through one's own effort and understanding, to build something in the space between two people — that does not change. Amateur radio has known this for a century. It will know it for a century more.

That, in the end, is why it exists. Not because it is useful, though it is. Not because it is technically interesting, though it is. But because the act at its centre is worth doing for its own sake. In a communication landscape that has made everything faster, cheaper and more convenient while quietly evacuating meaning from the exchange itself, that turns out to matter quite a lot.
